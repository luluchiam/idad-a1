ORB FARM

1. Information Design

Example:
The ecosystem simulation serves as the primary canvas for the visual presentation of information. Instead of using heavy text, users can see what's happening (such as fish movement, algae growth, and water level) through colour, motion, and subtle environmental changes.

My opinion of the Designer’s Rationale:
The designer prioritises minimalism and immersion, letting the system speak for itself. By avoiding information overload, it invites exploration and curiosity rather than instructing the user too directly.

Contrast:
The sidebar/tools panel uses clearer icons and organised menus for direct control, but the main simulation depends on visual data and intuitive learning. When put together, they produce a balance between presenting information in an exploratory and practical way.

2. Mapping

Example:
Each tool or element like soil, water, animals is mapped directly to interactive icons in the sidebar. Dragging and clicking have predictable, physical-like results, adding water raises the level, adding a fish makes it swim, etc.

My opinion of the Designer’s Rationale:
I think that the designer wants to mirror real-world logic without needing tutorials. Like science experiments or sandbox games, this physical mapping promotes an organic, experimentation interaction.

Contrast:
The tool icons provide clear features and consistent results, while interactions on the main canvas may seem less predictable like how a fish interacts with its environment, reflecting the complexity of nature vs. the simplicity of controls.

3. Characterisation

Example:
Elements in the simulation like fish, algae, or snails have subtle behaviors and appearances that give them “character.” Fish swim, algae grows, and particles float, creating a lively, dynamic world.

My opinion of the Designer’s Rationale:
The designer is trying to let the personality be shown through movement and interaction instead of text-based descriptions. This decision reflects a hands-off approach that encourages users to observe, deepening engagement through discovery.

Contrast:
The ecosystem characters are detailed and animated to feel alive, while the tool UI remains neutral and clean, deliberately avoiding character to keep the focus on the simulation.

4. Feedback

Example:
Feedback is mostly visual and dynamic. For example, when adding a plant, you immediately see it appear. If the water becomes dirty, it changes color. Ecosystem health is shown through natural visual shifts, not alerts.

My opinion of the Designer’s Rationale:
This non-verbal feedback style makes the experience organic and immersive. The designer wants users to watch and learn, not get pop-ups or gamey stats. It suits the meditative tone of the site.

Contrast:
Main simulation gives feedback through environmental change, which is slow and natural. Sidebar actions give immediate, clear feedback like an element appears or a tool is selected, which helps support the user in a more practical sense.

ADULT SWIM

1. Information Design

Example:
When you first open the site, you’re immediately dropped into a strange, glitchy 3D world with no instructions or labels. It’s filled with distorted shapes, pink blobs, hands, and weird textures. There’s no clear goal or guidance, the interface relies completely on visual impact and atmosphere to pull the user in.

My opinion of the Designer’s Rationale:
The lack of immediate text or instructions is a deliberate choice to disorient and immerse the user. The designer wants this to reflect a postmodern design attitude prioritising experience and emotion over clarity. This pushes the user to learn by interacting, encouraging them to treat the interface as a digital art installation, not a traditional website.

Contrast:
The main visual space (the distorted 3D world) uses heavy abstraction to convey atmosphere and mood with no explicit information. Any controls or triggers that appear later (such as when you interact or scroll) are more functional, but still minimal and artistically styled, continuing the ambiguity while guiding the user subtly.

2. Mapping

Example:
You interact by clicking, dragging, and flinging a limp ragdoll across the screen. Sound blocks are arranged in a rhythm grid, and when the ragdoll hits them, different sounds are triggered depending on how and where it collides. Every motion feels physical and has an immediate effect on the sound.

My opinion of the Designer’s Rationale:
The designer makes the mapping physical and intuitive. Dragging the ragdoll works like pulling a toy or puppet sort of mimics physics. The sound mapping uses spatial logic, where you hit affects how it sounds, creating a strong link between action and result.

Contrast:
The main canvas interactions (flinging the body) are exploratory and open-ended, while UI controls like the beat grid offer more structured, looped control like a music sequencer. One is chaotic and the other rhythmic.

3. Characterisation

Example:
The main “character” is a floppy, unconscious body that the user drags and throws through the space. It reacts with silly physics, bouncing and twisting around, giving it a strange, puppet-like personality. The surreal environment and exaggerated shapes add to its quirky and unsettling vibe.

My opinion of the Designer’s Rationale:
The designer wants the player’s avatar to be a performer and puppet. It's purposefully silly, making fun of control while allowing the character to express themselves through movement.

Contrast:
The ragdoll character is full of strange personality, while the grid and tools UI is sterile and graphic. The contrast helps focus emotional engagement on the main interactive element.

4. Feedback

Example:
The feedback comes through sound and motion. When the body hits a block, you hear a beat or melody that changes based on the location and speed of impact. The visual reactions like twitching, bouncing, and sound flashes immediately tell you that your action did something, even without words.

My opinion of the Designer’s Rationale:
The designer is tightly connecting sound and motion, creating a cause-and-effect loop that’s satisfying and encourages repeat experimentation. It avoids traditional UI feedback (like buttons or alerts), instead embedding responses in the experience itself.

Contrast:
The main canvas gives organic, audiovisual feedback that feels like performance art.
